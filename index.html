Conference paper: Dimensions

Abstract

            This article will guide you through the evolving concept of dimensions. We will begin by constructing our familiar world from the simplest possible elements,
            moving from a dimensionless point to the three-dimensional space we call home. From there, we will venture into the fourth dimension, exploring how Albert
            Einstein and Hermann Minkowski reshaped our understanding of reality by weaving space and time together into a single, dynamic fabric. We will take a leap into
            the abstract, using logic to imagine what higher spatial dimensions might be like. Finally, we will look at modern theories like string theory, which proposes
            that our universe contains hidden dimensions, not as a mathematical curiosity, but as a fundamental requirement for its existence.

Introduction

            The word "dimension" evokes images of parallel universes, yet in physics and mathematics, it denotes a fundamental degree of freedom: an independent coordinate
            required to specify a position in space. We intuitively navigate three spatial dimensions—left/right, forward/backwards, up/down—sufficient to locate any object,
            from a pen tip to a galactic core.
            Physicists, however, employ "dimension" in two interrelated senses. Spatial dimensions form the geometric structure of reality: orthogonal axes defining a manifold.
            Physical dimensions characterise measurable quantities in dimensional analysis—length (L), mass (M), time (T)—ensuring equations balance (e.g., velocity (V) as L/T).
